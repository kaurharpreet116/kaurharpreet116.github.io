How I Think as a Cloud Support Engineer

A practical workflow for triage, investigation, communication, and prevention.

Debug loop

  1. Confirm impact and scope, who is affected, what is failing, since when.
  2. Pick a failure domain (network, IAM, app, DNS, storage, OS).
  3. Check signals first (CloudWatch metrics, logs, ALB health, CloudTrail).
  4. Mitigate quickly with the lowest-risk change to restore service.
  5. Prove root cause using evidence, timelines, and change history.
  6. Prevent recurrence (alerts, runbooks, guardrails, checks).

Severity and communication

  • SEV 1: outage or major customer impact. Mitigate first, updates every 15 to 30 minutes.
  • SEV 2: partial outage or degraded performance. Restore critical path, then fix.
  • SEV 3: limited impact or workaround exists. Document and schedule fix.
  • Updates: what is confirmed, what is unknown, next step, and ETA for next update.
  • After: final summary, root cause, and prevention actions.

During incidents

Stay calm. Reduce blast radius. Restore service. Keep notes for the timeline.

Escalation

Escalate early when the issue crosses boundaries such as networking, security, or vendor dependencies.

Documentation

Impact, investigation, fix, prevention. Clear enough for the next engineer at 2 AM.

On-call and Support Mindset

Reliability is a habit. These are the behaviors I bring to production support.

Working under pressure

  • Restore service first with low-risk mitigation.
  • Keep a clear timeline of actions and observations.
  • Communicate simply and consistently during SEV incidents.
  • Escalate early when the incident crosses domain boundaries.

SLA and post-incident reviews

  • Think in uptime, error budgets, and customer impact.
  • After resolution, write RCA with evidence, not guesses.
  • Add prevention actions: alarms, guardrails, and runbook updates.
  • Reduce repeat tickets by improving documentation and detection.
